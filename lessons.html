Lessons Learned

Reflections on our project journey and key takeaways for future success

Partnership & Initial Contact

February 5, 2026

What Went Well

  • Partnership with SHP–NRV confirmed quickly through a brief phone call with Paul Mele
  • Full team attendance (Holden, Asher Tillman, Luis, Andres, Weijin Hsu) showed strong commitment
  • Paul was supportive and open to collaborating with the student team
  • Confirmed that existing SHP processes are acceptable for the course, reducing the need to create a new project from scratch

Challenges Encountered

  • The initial call was very short (10 minutes), leaving many operational details unaddressed
  • No specific project activities or roles were defined during this first contact
  • The team had limited knowledge of SHP’s build process and operational needs at this stage

What We Learned

  • A quick introductory call is effective for confirming partnership interest, but a longer in-person meeting is needed for detailed planning
  • The stakeholder (Paul Mele) values a structured project management approach with defined roles, timelines, and milestones
  • April 25 was identified early as the project end date, anchoring all future planning

PM Takeaway

Stakeholder Engagement: Early and direct contact with the primary stakeholder set a positive tone for the project. Understanding Paul’s expectation for structured project management helped align the team’s approach from the very beginning.

Kickoff Meeting & Project Scoping

February 9, 2026

What Went Well

  • In-depth face-to-face meeting at Panera Bread covered all key operational areas (funding, builds, delivery process)
  • Paul provided detailed insight into SHP’s funding model (local donors, churches, national distribution across 350 chapters)
  • Gained clear understanding of the bed build process (components, staining, mattress, bedding)
  • Two key dates established: April 10 (observation build) and April 25 (main build targeting 40 beds)
  • All team members present, no absences

Challenges Encountered

  • Several action items left with “TBD” deadlines (project timeline, lines of effort), requiring follow-up to set concrete dates
  • Volunteer recruitment plan still unresolved, dependent on sponsor needs not yet finalized
  • Storage logistics for collected bedding not yet determined
  • Communication structure (single point of contact) identified as needed but not yet established

What We Learned

  • Reverse planning from the April 25 build date is essential for identifying critical path activities
  • Project scope should focus on four “lines of effort”: manpower, materials, location, and logistics
  • Not all SHP builds are public; understanding the difference between closed and open builds is important for volunteer coordination
  • Bedding drive requires targeting 40 twin “bed-in-a-bag” sets, sourced from retailers like Amazon, Walmart, and Kohl’s
  • A single point of contact for communication with Paul is critical for clear, efficient coordination

PM Takeaway

Reverse Planning & Lines of Effort: Paul’s emphasis on working backwards from the April 25 build date provided a clear framework for milestone planning. Organizing work into distinct “lines of effort” (manpower, materials, location, logistics) gives the team a structured way to divide responsibilities and track progress independently across each area.
